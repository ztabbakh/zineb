1.1 Mechanics of a particle

Newton's second law of motion: There exist frame of reference in which the motion of the particle is described by the differential equation

\begin{align} \vec{F} &= \frac{d \vec{p}}{dt} = \dot{\vec{p}} \tag{1.3} \\ &= \frac{d}{dt} (mv) \tag{1.4} \end{align}

The reference frame mentioned above is called inertial or Galilian.

Conservation theorem for the linear momentum: If the total force, \(F\), is zero, then \(\dot{p} = 0\) and the linear momentum, \(p\), is conserved.

Angular Momentum about a point O

\begin{align*} \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} \end{align*}

Torque of moment of force about the point O.

\begin{align*} \vec{N} &= \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \\ &= \vec{r} \times \frac{d}{dt}(m \vec{v}) \\ &= \frac{d}{dt} (\vec{r} \times m \vec{v}) \\ &= \frac{d \vec{L} }{dt} \end{align*}

Conservation theorem for angular momentum of a particle: If the total torque, \(N\), is zero, then \(\dot{\vec{L}} = 0\), and the angular momentum is conserved.

Work done: By the external force \(F\) on the particle in going from point 1 to point 2

\begin{align*} W_{12} &= \int_1^2 F \cdot ds \\ &= \int_{1}^2 m \frac{dv}{dt} \cdot ds \\ &= \int_{1}^2 m \frac{dv}{dt} \cdot v dt \\ &= \frac{m}{2}\int_{1}^2 \frac{dv^2}{dt}dt \\ &= \frac{m}{2}\int_{1}^2 {dv^2} \\ &= \frac{mv_2^2}{2} - \frac{mv_2^1}{2} \\ &= T_2 - T_1 \\ &= \textrm{Change in kinetic energy} \end{align*}

Conservative forces

A conservative force F has a potential function V

\begin{align*} F \cdot ds = - dV \end{align*}

or

\begin{align*} F_s = - \frac{\p V}{\p s} \end{align*}

For a conservative system

\begin{align*} W_{12} = V_1 - v_2 \end{align*}

Therefore,

\begin{align*} T1 + V1 = T2 + V2 \end{align*

Energy conservation theorem for a particle: If the forces acting on a particle are conservative, then the total energy of the particle, \(T + V\), is conserved.

1.2 Mechanics of a system of particles

System of particle: external forces and internal forces

Newton's second law for the \(i\)-th particle

\begin{align*}\tag{1.19} ∑j Fji + Fi(e) = \dot{p}i \end{align*}

Rate of change of momentum of the \(i\)-th particle is equal to sum of forces external to the particle \(i\). These forces external to the \(i\)-th particles could be from other particle,\(F_{ji}\), or outside the system \(F_i^{(e)}\).

Q. What is weak law of action and reaction?

Summing Eq. (1.19) over all \(i\), and defining \(R = \frac{\sum m_i r_i}{\sum m_i}\) yields

\begin{align*} \tag{1.22} M \frac{d^2 R}{dt^2} = ∑i Fi(e) = F(e). \end{align*}

Mass times acceleration of the center of mass is equal to the sum of all external forces acting on the system. It is as if the system of particles is behaving like a particle positioned at the center of mass.

Conservation theorem for the linear momentum of a system of particles: If the total external force is zero, the total linear momentum is conserved.

1.2.1 Conservation of angular momentum for a system of particles

Angular momentum of the \(i\)-th particle is: \(r_i \times p_i\). Total angular momentum for the system is \(\sum_i (r_i \times p_i)\). Taking cross product of Eq. (1.19) with \(r_i\) on both side

\begin{align*} r_i \times \dot{p_i} = r_i \times F_i^{(e)} + \sum_j r_i \times F_{ji} \end{align*}

Summing over all \(i\)

\begin{align*} \sum_i (r_i \times \dot{p_i}) = \sum_i r_i \times F_i^{(e)} + \sum_{i,j ,i\neq j} r_i \times F_{ji} \end{align*}

Assuming strong law of action and reaction, all the torque from internal forces cancel each other out and we are left with

\begin{align*}\tag{1.26} \frac{d L}{dt} = N(e). \end{align*}

The time derivative of the total angular momentum is thus equal to the moment of the external force about the given point.

Linear momentum can be thought of as concentrated at the center of mass, but a similar result for angular momentum for a system of particles will have extra terms.

Let \(O\) be the reference origin. Write

\begin{align*} r_i = r_i' + R \end{align*}

differentiating both side

\begin{align*} v_i = v_i' + v \end{align*}

The total angular momentum of the system then is

\begin{align*}\tag{1.28} L &:= ∑i ri × mi vi
&= ∑i\left[ (ri' + R) × mi(vi' + v) \right] \\ &= ∑i ri'× mi vi' + ∑i ri'× miv + ∑i R × mi vi' + ∑i R × miv \\ &=R × Mv + ∑i ri'× pi' \end{align*}

Total angular momentum of a system of particles is the sum of the angular momentum of the center of mass about the origin plus the total angular momentum of the system about the center of mass.

1.2.2 Energy

Work done by all forces in moving a system of particles from configuration 1 to configuration 2:

\begin{align*} W_{12} &= \sum_i \int_1^2 F_i \cdot d s_i \\ \\ &=\sum_i \int_i^2 F_i^{(e)} \cdot ds_i + \sum_{i,j, i \neq j} \int_1^2 F _{ji} \cdot ds_i \\ &=\sum_i \int_1^2 (m_i v_i) \cdot v_i dt \\ &=\sum_i \int_1^2 d\left(\frac{1}{2} m_i v_i^2 \right) \\ & = T_2 - T_1 \end{align*}

Work done on a system of particles is equal to the difference between the final kinetic energy and the initial kinetic energy.

Making use of the transformation to center-of-mass coordinates, the kinetic energy

\begin{align*}\tag{1.31} T = \frac{1}{2} Mv2 + \frac{1}{2} ∑i mi vi'2 \end{align*}

which is it to say that kinetic energy of a system of particles is kinetic energy of the center of mass plus kinetic energy about the center of mass.

If the external forces and internal forces are both derivable from potentials is is possible to define a total potential energy, V, of the system,

\begin{align*}\tag{1.36} V = ∑i Vi + \frac{1}{2} ∑i,i,i≠ j Vij \end{align*} such that the total energy \(T +V\) is conserved.

1.3 Constraints

Holonomic constraint: If conditions of constraint can be expressed as equations connecting the coordinates of the particles (and possibly time) having the form

\begin{align*}\tag{1.37} f(r1,r2,r3,…,t) = 0 \end{align*}

then the constraints are said to be holonomic.

For example, rigid body constraint is an holonomic constraint. Equations are

\begin{align*} (r_i -r_j)^2 - c_{ij}^2 = 0. \end{align*}

Constraint without any equations are nonholonomic. For example, gas molecules confined to a room.

1.4 D'Alembert's Principle and Lagrange's Equations

A virtual (infinitesimal) displacement of a system refers to a change in the configuration of the system as the result of any arbitrary infinitesimal change of the coordinates \(\dt r_i\), consistent with the forces and constraints imposed on the given instant \(t\). A virtual displacement is imagined displacement, not actually happening.

System is in equilibrium if \(F_i = 0\) for all \(i\). Therefore, virtual work on the i the particle \(F \cdot \dt r_i = 0\). The total work will also vanish.

\begin{align*}\tag{1.40} ∑i Fi ⋅ \dt ri = 0 \end{align*}

Nothing new has been introduced yet. Decompose \(F_i = F_i^{(a)} + f_i\), into applied force and constraint force.

Eq. (1.40) also splits

\begin{align*} \tag{1.42} ∑i Fi(a) ⋅ \dt ri + ∑i fi ⋅ \dt ri = 0 \end{align*}

We now restrict ourselves to systems for which the net virtual work of the forces of constraint is zero. For example: rigid body,

Principle of virtual work for a system of particles: Virtual work of applied forces vanishes:

\begin{align*}\tag{1.43} ∑i Fi(a) ⋅ \dt ri = 0 \end{align*}

Eq. (1.43) only gives condition for static equilibrium. We need little more to account for motion. This we get by D'Alembert's principle:

D'Alembert's principle

If the particles are in motion, for each particle equation of motion is true

\begin{align*} F_i^{(a)} + f_i = \dot{p}_i \end{align*}

Basically, when particles are in motion, it is the sum of applied force, constraint force and the inertial forces that vanish and the total virtual work from them should vanish.

\begin{align*} \sum_i (F_i^{(a)} + f_i - \dot{p}_i) \cdot \dt r_i = 0 \end{align*}

Again, assuming that the net internal force by the constrain force is zero we arrive at the D'Alembert's Principle.

\begin{align*}\tag{1.45} ∑i (Fi(a) - \dot{p}i) ⋅ \dt ri = 0 \end{align*}

This principle in its present still does not give solvable equations of motion. Generalized coordinates need to be brought it.

Suppose we have generalized coordinates: \(q_1,q_2, ..., q_n\) and we have the dependence

\begin{align*} r_i = r_i (q_1, q_2, ..., q_n, t) \end{align*}

Chain rule

\begin{align*}\tag{1.46} vi := \frac{dr_i }{dt} = ∑k \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_k} \dot{q}k + \frac{\p r_i}{\p t} \end{align*}

Arbitrary virtual displacement \(\dt r_i\) expressed in terms of generalized coordinates

\begin{align*} \dt r_i = \sum_k \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_q} \dt q_k \end{align*}

Virtual work in terms of the generalized coordinates

\begin{align*} \sum_i f_i \cdot \dt r_i = \sum_{i,j} F_i \cdot \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} \dt q_j \end{align*} \begin{align*} =\sum_j \underbrace{\left(\sum_i F_i \cdot \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j }\right)}_{:= Q_j}\dt q_j \end{align*}

Generalized force: \(Q_j\)

\(Q_j\) and \(\dt q_i\) may not have dimension of force or displacement, but their product will have the dimension of work.

Now we turn our attention to the inertial force term in the D'Alembert's principle

\begin{align*} \sum_i \dot{p}_i \cdot \dt r_i &= \sum_i m_i \ddot{r}_i \cdot \dt r_i \\ &= \sum_{ij} m_i \ddot{r}_i \cdot \frac{\dt r_i}{\dt q_j} \dt q_j \end{align*}

We have

\begin{align*} \sum_i m_i \ddot{r}_i \cdot \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} = \sum_i \left[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( m_i \dot{r}_i \cdot \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} \right) - m_i \dot{r}_i \cdot \frac{d}{dt} \left(\frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} \right) \right] \end{align*}

Interchanging the order of differentiation in the last term of the above equation

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} \right) &= \frac{\p \dot{r}_i}{\p q_j} = \frac{\p }{\p q_j} \left(\sum_k \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_k} \dot{q}_k + \frac{\p r_i}{\p t} \right) \\ &= \frac{\p v_i}{\p q_j} \end{align*}

It seems that \(\dot{q}_j\)-s are independent from each other. Therefore, differentiating Eq (1.46) gives

\begin{align*}\tag{1.51} \frac{\p v_i}{\p \dot{q}j} = \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} \end{align*}

Continuing from Eq. (1.50)

\begin{align*} \sum_i m_i \ddot{r}_i \cdot \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} &= \sum_i \left[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( m_i \dot{r}_i \cdot \frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} \right) - m_i \dot{r}_i \cdot \frac{d}{dt} \left(\frac{\p r_i}{\p q_j} \right) \right] \\ &= \sum_i \left[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( m_i v_i \cdot \frac{\p v_i}{\p \dot{q}_j} \right) - m_i v_i \cdot \left(\frac{\p v_i}{\p q_j} \right) \right] \end{align*} \begin{align*} \sum_i \dot{p}_i \cdot \dt r_i &= \sum_i m_i \ddot{r}_i \cdot \dt r_i \\ &= \sum_{ij} m_i \ddot{r}_i \cdot \frac{\dt r_i}{\dt q_j} \dt q_j \\ &= \sum_j \left( \sum_i m_i \ddot{r}_i \cdot \frac{\dt r_i}{\dt q_j} \right)\dt q_j \\ &= \sum_j \left( \sum_i \left[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( m_i v_i \cdot \frac{\p v_i}{\p \dot{q}_j} \right) - m_i v_i \cdot \left(\frac{\p v_i}{\p q_j} \right) \right] \right)\dt q_j \\ &=\sum_j \left\{ \frac{d}{dt}\left[\frac{\p}{\p \dot{q}_j} \left(\sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2 \right) \right] -\frac{\p}{\p q_j} \left( \sum_i \frac{1}{2} m_i v_i^2 \right) \right\} \dt q_j \end{align*}

Defining \(T =\frac{1}{2} \sum_i m_i v_i^2\) as the system kinetic energy, D'Alembert's principle becomes

\begin{align*}\tag{1.52} ∑j \left\{\left[\frac{d}{dt} \left(\frac{\p T}{\p \dot{q}j} \right) - \frac{\p T}{\p q_j} \right]- Qj \right\} \dt qj = 0 \end{align*}

We will aim to look for conditions which require Eq. (1.52) to vanish term by term.

If forces come from a potential function \(V\), we get

\begin{align*} Q_j = - \frac{\p V}{\p q_j} \end{align*}

Then Eq. (1.53) becomes

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\p T}{\p \dot{q}_j} \right) - \frac{\p(T - V)}{\p q_j} = 0 \end{align*}

Under some conditions, \(V\) can be introduced in the first derivatives

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\p (T - V)}{\p \dot{q}_j} \right) - \frac{\p(T - V)}{\p q_j} = 0 \end{align*}

Defining Lagrangian \(L = T - V\) we get the "Lagrange's equation"

\begin{align}\tag{1.57} \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\p L}{\p \dot{q}j} \right) - \frac{\p L}{\p q_j} = 0 \end{align}

1.5 Velocity-dependent potentials and the dissipation function

In the previous section we observed that if the force functions are gradient of some potential \(V\) then we get a Lagrangian equation (1.57).

Consider a charge, \(q\), of mass \(m\) moving at a velocity, \(v\), in an otherwise charge-free region containing both an electric field, \(E\), and a magnetic field \(B\), which may depend upon time and position. Charge experience a force called Lorentz force, given by

\begin{align*} F = q [E + (v \times B)] \end{align*}

Suppose there exist a scalar potential \(\phi(t,x,y,z)\) and a vector potential \(A(t,x,y,z)\) such that

\begin{align*} E &= - \nl \phi - \frac{\p A}{\p t} \\ B &= \nl \times A \end{align*}

Potential function \(U\) comes out to be

\begin{align*} U = q \phi - q A \cdot v \end{align*}

Lagrangian

\begin{align*} L &:= T - V \\ &= \frac{1}{2} m v^2 - q \phi + q A \cdot v \end{align*}

1.6.1 Single particle in space: using Cartesian coordinates

Generalized forces are \(F_x, F_y, F_z\).

\begin{align*} T = \frac{1}{2} m (\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2 + \dot{z}^2) \end{align*}

Are we assuming that \(x\) and \(\dot{x}\) are independent while deriving

\begin{align*} \frac{\p T}{\p x} =\frac{\p T}{\p y} =\frac{\p T}{\p z} = 0 ?? \end{align*} \begin{align*} \frac{\p T}{\p \dot{x}} = m \dot{x}, \quad \frac{\p T}{\p \dot{y}} = m \dot{y}, \quad \frac{\p T}{\p \dot{z}} = m \dot{z} \end{align*}

Therefore we get the equations of motion

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}(m \dot{x}) = F_x, \quad \frac{d}{dt}(m \dot{y}) = F_y, \quad \frac{d}{dt}(m \dot{z}) = F_z \end{align*}

Which is original Newton's law.

1.6.2 Single particle: using polar coordinates

\begin{align*} x = r \cos \theta, \quad y = r \sin \theta \end{align*} \begin{align*} \dot{x} &= \dot{r} \cos \theta - r \dot{\theta} \sin \theta \\ \dot{y} &= \dot{r} \sin \theta + r \dot{\theta} \cos \theta \end{align*}

The kinetic energy \(T = \frac{1}{2} m v^2\) then reduces to

\begin{align*} T = \frac{1}{2} m \left[ \dot{r}^2 + (r \dot{\theta})^2\right] \end{align*}

Recall Eq. (1.49) for the definition of the generalized forces

\begin{align*}\tag{1.49} Qj = ∑i Fi ⋅ \frac{\p r_i}{ \p q_j} \end{align*}

\begin{align*} dr = \hat{r} dr + r \hat{\theta} d \theta + \hat {k} dz \end{align*} \begin{align*} Q_r = F \cdot \frac{\p r}{\p r} = F \cdot \hat{r} = F_r \end{align*} \begin{align*} Q_{\theta} = F \cdot \frac{\p r}{\p \theta} = F \cdot r \hat{\theta} = r F_{\theta} \end{align*}
